Reality

How We Got Here

Power didn't accidentally concentrate. It was structured, century by century. Scroll the timeline and expand any moment.

Ancient → Medieval

Codifying property & descent

Legal codes tied land, inheritance, and legal personhood to men, making economic power heritable through the male line.

This didn't reflect a natural order — it was a choice encoded in law, repeated until it felt inevitable. Matrilineal systems survived where these codes didn't reach. [anthro]

15th–19th c.

Colonial imposition of gender norms

Colonial powers often dismantled matrilineal and matrifocal systems, imposing European patriarchal law over existing equitable structures.

Many societies the colonizers encountered already distributed power more evenly. Imposing "civilization" frequently meant concentrating it. [anthro]

19th c.

The public/private split

Industrialization drew a hard line between paid "public" work and unpaid "private" care — assigning the latter to women and making it invisible.

Unpaid care still props up every economy. Its invisibility is a structural reason "women's work" stays undervalued and undercounted. [ILO]

1893–present

Suffrage & representation waves

From New Zealand in 1893 to ongoing struggles, the vote expanded — but representation lagged far behind the ballot.

A century after many won the vote, women hold just 27.2% of parliamentary seats globally. The vote opened the door; shared power still hasn't walked through. [IPU 2025]

20th–21st c.

Quotas & parity experiments

Electoral gender quotas and parity rules began reshaping who gets to decide — with measurable results.

Rwanda's parliament is ~61% women; several nations sit near 50%. Structural rules, not goodwill, drove the shift. [IPU Parline]
