Patriarchy has a beginning — around 3100 BCE in Mesopotamia. If it was built, it can be unbuilt. Explore the historical evidence, the societies that defied the trend, and the levers that could reshape the future.

Part I — The Forensic Descent

How Patriarchy Was Constructed

Patriarchy is not a biological inevitability. It emerged over 2,500 years through agriculture, property law, and the codification of gender hierarchy. Trace the layers — from egalitarian origins to rigid law codes.

Part II — The Matrilineal Atlas

Societies That Defied the Trend

No true matriarchy — women dominating men as a group — has ever been documented. But matrilineal societies have thrived for millennia. Click any card to reveal the "matrilineal puzzle": where descent flowed through women, but power often stayed with men.

Key distinction: Matrilineal descent (inheritance through mothers) ≠ matriarchy (women's political dominance). No true matriarchy — as a mirror-image of patriarchy — has ever been documented. The societies shown here are matrilineal or matrifocal, not matriarchal in the strict sense. The Minangkabau, as argued by Peggy Reeves Sanday, come closest to a form of matriarchy where women are central to economic, religious, and social life without dominating men.
