# Research Brief: Why the World Isn't a Matriarchy & Ways of Changing That

## 1. KEY DISTINCTIONS (Critical for accuracy)

### Matriarchy ≠ Matriliny ≠ Matrifocality
- **Matriarchy**: A social system where positions of power/authority are held by women; descent and relationship reckoned through the female line (OED definition). Most academics hold that no unambiguously matriarchal society (completely excluding men from authority) has been documented.
- **Matriliny**: Descent traced through the female line. Only ~17% of societies in the Standard Cross-Cultural Sample are matrilineal, vs. ~41% patrilineal. Matriliny does NOT mean women rule — men often hold authority as brothers/uncles rather than as fathers/husbands.
- **Matrifocality**: Mother-centered households; does not necessarily imply female political rule.
- **Matriarchy ≠ "inverted patriarchy"**: Scholars like Peggy Reeves Sanday and Heide Göttner-Abendroth argue matriarchies are egalitarian, consensus-based, gift-economies — NOT women dominating men.

## 2. WHY MATRIARCHIES ARE RARE — Key Explanations from Anthropology

### A. Subsistence & Economic Transitions
- **Horticulture favors matriliny; plough agriculture/pastoralism favors patriliny.** Aberle (1961): "the cow is the enemy of matriliny, and the friend of patriliny." When societies adopt cattle, ploughs, or intensive agriculture, wealth concentrates in male hands, undermining matriliny.
- Holden & Mace (2003): Phylogenetic analysis of Bantu societies showed adopting cattle led to loss of matrilineal descent.
- Market economies and wage labor also undermine matriliny by concentrating wealth in men and breaking down lineage structures in favor of nuclear families (Gough 1961).

### B. Colonialism
- Colonial powers imposed patrilineal succession, male primogeniture, and patriarchal legal systems. Shenk et al. (2019): "transitions away from matriliny have been significantly more common than reverse transitions" and this may be "an artefact of the colonial and globalizing period."
- Colonial governments preferred unambiguous male succession because it made governance simpler.
- Missionaries and religious conversion further eroded matrilineal practices.

### C. The "Matrilineal Puzzle"
- Audrey Richards (1950): In matrilineal societies, men are torn between authority over their sisters' children (their heirs) and their own wives' children. This creates structural tension that can push toward patriliny.
- Murdock (1949): Matriliny combined with patrilocal residence is "unstable and unmistakably transitional."

### D. Warfare
- Internal warfare (with neighboring same-society groups) is associated with patriliny; external warfare with matriliny. When warfare patterns shift, kinship systems can shift too (Ember & Ember 1971).

### E. Social Complexity & State Formation
- Patrilineal descent better concentrates power in a vertical line, facilitating long-lasting large-scale empires. Matriliny diffuses power sideways, making it harder to consolidate (Douglas 1969, Duindam 2021).
- No large consolidated empire practiced matrilineal descent at the ruling-house level.

### F. World Religions
- Major religions (Islam, Christianity, Buddhism, Confucianism) consolidated existing biases against female rule in their sacred texts and teachings, further marginalizing women from political authority over centuries.

## 3. LIVING/RECENT MATRILINEAL & MATRIARCHAL SOCIETIES (Examples for the website)

| Society | Location | Key Features |
|---------|----------|--------------|
| **Minangkabau** | West Sumatra, Indonesia | ~4 million people; world's largest matrilineal society; women own land/houses, descent through female line. Sanday calls it a "modern matriarchy." |
| **Mosuo** | Yunnan, China | Matrilineal, matrilocal; women head households; "walking marriages"; tourism is changing their culture. |
| **Khasi** | Meghalaya, India | Matrilineal; youngest daughter inherits ancestral property; men move to wives' homes. |
| **Hopi** | Arizona, USA | Matrilineal clans, matrilocal, matrifocal households; gender-egalitarian; women own houses and land. |
| **Iroquois (Haudenosaunee)** | NE North America | Clan Mothers held significant political power; selected chiefs; could veto war. |
| **Akan/Ashanti** | Ghana | Matrilineal descent; queen-mothers as political actors; "destoolment" of chiefs. |
| **Umoja** | Kenya | All-female village founded 1995; women fleeing gender-based violence. |

## 4. ARGUMENTS FOR CHANGE — "Ways of Changing That"

### A. Political/Institutional
- More women in legislatures correlates with: decreased corruption, more laws on sexual harassment/rape/domestic violence, increased social welfare spending.
- Quotas and proportional representation systems increase women's political representation.
- The Iroquois model: Clan Mothers selecting and removing leaders offers a template for accountability.

### B. Economic
- Subsistence/gift economies (Göttner-Abendroth): Matriarchal economies are based on circulation of gifts, not accumulation. Regional subsistence economies as alternative to global capitalism.
- Women's control over land and resources (as in Minangkabau, Hopi) as basis for economic equality.
- Equal distribution of national wealth: 50% to women's projects, 50% to men's (Göttner-Abendroth's proposal).

### C. Social/Cultural
- Consensus-based decision-making from clan level upward (Göttner-Abendroth's "egalitarian societies of consensus").
- Redefining matriarchy not as "women ruling over men" but as egalitarian, needs-oriented, non-hierarchical social organization.
- Challenging patriarchal norms through education, media, entertainment-education (e.g., Population Media Center's radio dramas).

### D. Spiritual/Cultural
- Re-sacralization of nature and the feminine divine as an alternative to hierarchical, transcendent religious structures.
- Indigenous movements preserving matriarchal patterns and elements.

### E. Feminist Thought
- First-wave: Stanton, Gage, Gilman advocated women-centered governance.
- Second-wave: Cultural feminism, Goddess movement, Göttner-Abendroth's Modern Matriarchal Studies.
- Contemporary: Maija West's "matriarchal governance design" — applying matriarchal principles to modern organizational structures.

## 5. KEY STATISTICS & FACTS FOR THE WEBSITE

- Of 1,291 populations in the Ethnographic Atlas: 160 matrilineal, 590 patrilineal, 362 bilateral, 52 duolateral, 50 mixed, 48 ambilineal.
- Transitions away from matriliny: 35% (liberal) to 13.3% (conservative) of SCCS societies. Reverse transitions to matriliny: 11.1% (liberal) to 1.6% (conservative).
- Matriliny is positively associated with: horticulture, matrilocal residence, matrilineal inheritance, absence of large domestic animals, absence of plough cultivation.
- Women in parliaments globally: growing but still minority. As of 2024, women hold ~27% of parliamentary seats worldwide (IPU data).
- Men are ~3x more likely to die by suicide — linked by some scholars to toxic masculine norms (patriarchy harms men too).
